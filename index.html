Statement

In 2019, I visited a lunchtime lecture about making a typeface. The lecturer introduced his ‘brand-new’ typeface, which is a revival of historical drawings. As I’ve designed typefaces, I had questions about how he dealt with license issues of the original drawing in his country. He kindly answered my questions and said “Every type designer is a thief.” As a type designer, this has strongly stuck with me. I questioned myself. Would it be called theft if I sneak around handwriting, lettering, or open-source fonts to sketch my typeface and get inspiration from their shapes at some point? At what moment would the theft occur? I believe I’m not a thief, but on second thought, now I’m not so sure.

I might be a thief. I peer into font files and compare them with my typeface to figure out how I can make mine different from others. As I continue this process, I get used to typefaces designed by others, remember the shapes, and internalize them. I might unconsciously use this memory when I create a new typeface. The borders between my original sketch and other designs get blurry. Do I affirm that this is not theft? Drawing from internalized memory seems in the spectrum of stealing. Copyright license aims to protect digital contents from piracy, but honestly who cares? Who is actually monitoring or tracking people stealing a font file? Persistent theft has loosed my morals and conscience. In the meantime, I found a fair way of stealing. Unlike copyright, the copyleft license allows people to copy and modify font files. So I downloaded an open-source typeface, UniFont, customized it, and created the new typeface, Pirata Mono. Under the copyleft, theft is fair play and even encouraged. I was excited that I found the way of not feeling guilty about stealing other typefaces. And the innocent stealing was the seed of something new.

I have to say I am a thief. I keep stealing licensed e-books, images, and all kinds of metadata. I press command c and v, or right-click images to download. Digital theft has become indispensable and even rampant in my digital labor. But I realize what I’ve stolen is not the materials, but someone else’s labor. To steal the shape of the letter “S” in MetaFont, it takes seconds and is just too easy to hijack millions hours of digital labor by Donald Knuth. The convenient stealing makes the value of digital labor trifling and, moreover, unknowable. The digital Labor Recorder that I’ve created captures the unrepresented digital labor and reveals it in real-time.

Sometimes I should’ve stolen something from me. Sound has been an annoying subject to me. When Paul Elliman asked me what music I like, I couldn’t answer because I don’t like listening to music. I had to create sound, but I knew nothing about sound. I came up with imaginary sound that I haven't ever heard. Something like the sound of metadata. I looked around the most familiar sound to copy. It was MY sound. In the sound of metadata, ‘The breathing’ or ‘When I’m thinking’ is the mimicked sound that I observed and replicated my habit of speaking and mumbling.

I will be a thief. I first recognized myself as a thief when making typefaces, but theft has been complicated through experiencing graphic design, coding, and sound. Theft is crafty, enterprising and more than just stealing. The nature of theft is entangled with originality, value, labor, protection, substitution, quality, and branches out further like vines. By crossing over various disciplines, my practice are the exploration of how I perceive theft, why I steal, and how I exploit copied materials.

Pirata

Pirata

Typeface, 2020-21
Pirata Mono Light, Regular, Regular Italic, Regular Proportional

Pirata Mono is a customized font based on Unifont. Pirata mono trial is under copyleft licensing, which is to give all user the freedom to download, customize, share the design free. For the mashers, modders, remakers who use digital cut-and-paste technologies, copyleft allows them to make something new from something old. Pirata Mono Trial has some missing characters represented ‘⟲’. But people use entire character set or won't be interrupted, if buy the full version.



Process

A font file is now the most vulnerable digital material to theft. This is because the file is unencrypted, allowing people to copy accurate coordinate values of shapes and typography information. As I acknowledge the weakness of the protection, I’ve tried not to copy, share or modify font files made by other designers when using fonts. When making fonts, however, there are times when I inevitably open other font files and check coordinate values of shapes.

I peer into multiple typefaces which look similar to my typeface and compare them with my design to find out how I can make mine more characteristic and different from others. As the comparison continues, I get used to the shapes designed by others, remember the shapes, and internalize them. In this process, this memory can be unconsciously withdrawn when I create a new typeface, and the borders between my original sketch and other designs get blurry. Do I affirm this is not theft? It seems that drawing from internalized memory is kind of in the spectrum of stealing. We know that copyright license aims to protect digital contents from piracy, but honestly who cares? Who is actually monitoring or tracking people stealing a font file?

In the meantime, I found the fair way of stealing in type design. The legal and innocent stealing is possible under the ‘copyleft’ license.[1] The concept of the copyleft is ‘all wrongs reserved’. Unlike copyright, users can freely steal digital contents and shake-up or troll them under the copyleft. Maybe the original contents must get washed away while modifications, but it can be the way of creating a new contents from old ones. In this internet free culture, the copyleft is originated from the belief that openness and collaboration can generate more qualified software, rather than preserving the original data or forbidding piracy. In this copyleft scenario, type designers can fairly steal ideas or shapes to make a new typeface.

Pirata[2] is the typeface under copyleft that anyone can download, customize the design, and share for free. Unlike most trial fonts, any modifications, shake-ups, or trolling are allowed as well as selling others the secondary works. While copyright prohibits sharing and modification of digital contents, copyleft license is originated from the belief that transparency, openness, and publicity can generate more qualified software, revitalizing the software market. Not only for users, copyleft can be a breakthrough for some type designers who want to be free from the pressure of protecting their design. Although the originality gets washed away through modifying under copyleft, that would be another opportunity to remember the original shape of contents, and the original creator’s name can be recorded into the credit of modified content.

In this context, Pirata is the manifesto of stealing as protection against copyright license policy. Pirata is modified from the pixel font, Unifont, which is an open-source font and had been widely used by embedding to freeware software in the '90s. Like other trial fonts, Pirata Mono has missing characters, ‘C, O, P, Y, L, E, F, T’, and the shape of missing characters are substituted by the logo of copyleft. In ‘piratamonotrial.download’, Pirata Mono Trial’s online showcase, users can test and download it. Following the copyleft manifesto, the concept of the website was inspired by the interface of old font-making programs.

Additionally, I offer the full version of Pirata Mono for users who simply want to use the font to their design instead of modifying the shape. In the full version, it has all characters like numerals or symbols to function as a practical typeface. Now I'm adding more typeface families such as Pirata Mono Italic, Pirata Mono Light, and Pirata Regular and plan to support multi- languages like Cyrillic, Greek, and Korean.

Pirata Mono will follow the spirit of copyleft. Trialfontexpert.com triggered me to suss out the problematic point of protecting typeface within copyright and was a driving force to make Pirata and piratamonotrial.download. As Unifont allowed me to ‘steal’ it, Pirata Mono is a configurable typeface, allowing users to utilize it without constraints by piracy. Especially in type design area, this practice is expected to be the discourse of how fair use of stealing can be the protection by a typeface designer.

Trial Font Archive

Trial Font Archive

Research, website, 2020
trialfontexpert.com

This is a website that has a name list of trial fonts over 3,400 and their terms of use. As a trial font is a demo of its full version, everyone can download it for free. Type foundries have promoted their typeface by offering trial fonts, and users have downloaded them to test directly to designs before users buy the full version. As I haven’t seen or offered a trial font in Korea, a trial font is such a curiosity and challenging material in terms of protecting design. To find out the license condition of various trial fonts, I’ve downloaded trial files and their Terms of Use as many as possible.



Process

The concept of trialfontexpert.com is an encyclopedia that archives vast information of trial fonts in a single webpage. Especially on the collection of terms of use page, core contents are highlighted and quickly viewed with navigators. The webpage reminds font users how common trial fonts are and both the file and design are unprotected from theft. Trial fonts are hard to get free from theft no matter how hard type designers protect them. It’s because that the files don’t automatically expire and have no additional technology that can force users to stop using the files. Especially in the part of sharing, copying, and modifying in Terms of Use written by many type foundries, type foundry appeals to the user’s conscience. The more problematic is that every type foundries has slightly different or ambiguous licensing policies. But honestly, who cares about the Terms of Use? Even I have never written or read Terms of Use for my typefaces.

Despite the constant threat from theft, a trial font has the minimal protection planted by the type designer. It’s called ‘missing characters.’ Unlike a full version, a trial font often has only lowercase and uppercase and doesn’t contain numerals, symbols, which can reduce the risk of exposing the entire design in the first place. In trialfontexpert.com, all texts are set by the trial font, Monument Grotesk Semi-mono Trial by Dinamo, and many characters are missing and are replaced by question marks (�). Each missing character can be any shape other than question marks, so a typeface designer can actively utilize the missing characters as design components when drawing the forms. That being said a designer can decide how much or how to protect the font design, at least in making a trial font.

Digital Labor Recorder

Digital Labor Recorder

Website, Digital tool, 2020
digitallaborrecorder.live

Digital Labor Recorder tracks all keyboard events that appear during digital labor in real time. The purpose of digital labor is to make digital materials (for example, jpg or pdf files), using digital devices. Unlike physical labor, digital labor can be done in any location, without time constraints. But it remains unknown who is working beyond the screen and how much time and effort they are exerting. For example, we barely witness the evidence of labor for type design, one of the most time-consuming digital labor processes. Similarly, a font file can be shown as flat and partial metadata and fails to represent the process.

At the keyboard, a digital worker’s hands and tools physically and constantly meet to render digital materials. Through this website, we can see the process of digital labor through the tying of keys on the keyboard that is constantly updated in real time, and the log is recorded as evidence of digital labor even after the work is finished. Although recorded key events are still seemingly fragmentary metadata, this website allows one to witness digital labor and would be a starting point to think about how to detect the unknowable process of digital labor.



Process

As there was much time to stay at home and work due to COVID-19, I’ve been working on designing Hangul (Korean letter system) typeface over summer break. When making one character, I saw there are 255 nodes and points to be adjusted for one single glyph. Hangul has 11,172 characters in total, which implies that I have to refine millions of nodes and points and it takes approximately one or two years to be published. After nine month I finally generated the font, and the file was 323KB. As a font file is a digital product, I can set the price to sell it but haven’t decided how much yet. Considering the average font price in a market, my font would be $35-65. But some questions arise. Does the digital material described as 323KB and $50 represent the thousands hours of my digital labor? How are people aware of the value of my digital labor on making the font? More fundamentally, what is the value of digital material? As Marx said, labor process is value-making process. Perhaps it’s helpful to know digital labor process to detect the value of digital materials.

Donald Knuth, a computer scientist and a professor at Stanford University, made a MetaFont in 1970s. Because Knuth was an outsider of graphic industry, he calculated all the shapes of letters in mathmetic way and constructed it by coding. While making MetaFont, he mentioned about the difficulty of making the letter ’S’ in interviews. Additionally, the frustration of making ’S’ can be seen on his work journals. Knuth wrote one journal on Jun 30 1970, and it says “Up late Wednesday, tried again. For eight hours to finish the letter ’S’. Not feeling very good, will not go onto this kind of goofy schedule any more.” After he finished making MetaFont, he wrote a paper, ‘The Letter S’. It describes how he constructed the shape and the detailed making-process. The external ‘S’ seems just flat dry metadata. However through the additional record like interviews, journals and the paper, I could re-recognize the value of S-looking like graphic and empathize eight hours’ digital labor by Knuth. Digital workers are unseen, but digital labor process is describable and representable.

But have you ever “witnessed” someone doing digital labor? Recently people have streamed in real time what they are doing and how they work on Youtube and Instagram. Does real-time streaming make their outcomes more special? Or do people expect another rewards or compensation for their digital labor?

Think about the simplest type of digital labor, I email people, read e-books, and write my thoughts to you from Korea remotely. Digital labor is easy and convenient to do. I even don’t need to say something out load. All I need to do is just to press a keyboard, siting on a chair. In contrast, the moment of digital labor is unknownable. Digital workers are always beyond the screen, and only witnesser of labor process is the digital worker themselves.

Meanwhile, digital tools have recorded my labor in real time. At keyboard, my hands and digital tool meet and interact constantly. Keys are pressed and released. They are delivering my inputs, dematerializing my physical labor. And dematerialized input logs are re-recorded as written texts at the bottom of this website. Like Donald Knuth’s journals and paper are seemingly the clue for picturing his digital labor process, the recorded keyboard events at the bottom of this website would be the evidence of my digital labor process. The keyboard events history that you and I just saw could be the partial record of my 3-minute-digital labor. But the record is limited to show the movement of a digital tool. The text still has no capacity to represent who was the digital worker and how the worker was ‘moving’ around. A digital worker’s eyes, hands motion, and sound are still unrepresented. If some records of digital labor process are lacking of humanity, how I determine the digital labor is rendered by me or AI? In the world that AI and human work together, I keep detecting the digital labor process rendered by human, which is the value-making process.

A web cam is another digital tool that captures someone’s physical movement. Due to pandemic, I’ve been using a web cam a lot and often more than before. Especially on Zoom we can see people moving around our faces, flickering eyes, or waiving hands. Speaking of Zoom’s meeting room interface, it’s quite awkward and annoying that I can see my own face as well. A web cam is capturing my own physical motion that even I haven’t noticed. Camera, real time body movement, and digital labor. A webcam is likely to punctuate like a motion capture device. If a webcam can track face and hand movements, it might be a new tool to detect a digital labor process. By using a webcam and some coding, I created a secondary digital labor detector. I visited a new digital labor detector, access microphone and camera. And then it’s all set to detect digital labor. When I’m trying to draw a typeface, I witnessed my eye’s flickering, turning my head around, mouse clicking sounds, fingers motions, and heart rate at once. After a few minutes digital labor, I got the letter ‘s’. But I got all the labor sceneries that my web cam had captured. With is detector, the ‘S’ rendered on the workplace is not the only product of this digital labor. All scenes re bi-products and remain as the animated evidence of making the letter ‘S’.

Left-handedness

Left-handedness

Digital book, 2019
138 x 210 x 20mm

The looking of a left hand is the symmetry of a right hand, but a left hand has been differentiated in symbolic, religious, and historical ways. In some contexts, a left hand is treated as an unjust, prejudicial distinction, or feminine. while a right hand means something right, origin, or masculine. From the perspective of a left-handed, this book follows what a left hand distinguishes a right hand, and how a left hand and a right hand reflect each other in many different cultures.

The book has fourteen texts, from three different books, The New Ambidextrous Universe by Martin Gardner, Right & left; essays on dual symbolic classification by Rodney Needham, and The Right Hand: Left-Handedness by Daniel Wilson. For the design part, all body texts are placed on the left-handed pages. On the left-handed pages, there are some keywords flipped from left-handed pages so that two pages mirror and show each other.
